# 78% of companies use AI. Only 1% of executives call their rollouts mature.

**78% of organizations now use AI** in at least one business function. **Only 1% of C-suite executives describe their GenAI rollouts as "mature."** The dominant pattern among the rest: **cost savings under 10% per function and revenue gains under 5%** *(Stanford AI Index 2025; McKinsey survey, N=2,854).*

That gap isn't an adoption problem. It's a value-creation problem — and most leaders are looking in the wrong place to fix it.

Walk through any mid-market business in 2026 and you'll find the same paradox. Everyone says they want innovation. Everyone agrees AI is changing the rules. And almost nobody has the slack to actually do the work that would unlock either.

Why? Because your people are running the shop. They're keeping the systems alive that someone, at some point, designed for an era when the bottlenecks were different. The processes outlived the bottlenecks. And the value that *would* show up — if anyone had the bandwidth to look — sits exactly in the places they cannot reach.

This piece is about where that value lives, why your team can't see it, and what to do on Monday morning to start.

## The trap: running the shop

A lot of work in any 2026 business is **maintenance work**. Re-running last quarter's report. Reformatting last week's deck. Reconciling last month's invoices. Filling the same regulatory forms with the same fields. None of it is bad. All of it is necessary. Most of it is structured around an old scarcity — engineering hours, designer time, analyst attention, advisor capacity — that's no longer what binds.

> **DATA — The 9% gap**
>
> Megan Reitz and John Higgins surveyed 3,000+ employees for HBR Digital (2025). **73% of executives recognized curiosity and imagination as critical** to their organization. **Only 9% of employees felt their leaders supported those traits.** That gap isn't ill will. It's structural. The same processes leaders preach against — the ones that suppress curiosity — are the processes their organizations run on. The leaders are protecting their own bottleneck.
>
> *Source: Reitz & Higgins, "Spacious Thinking," HBR Digital, July 2025.*

Most workers can articulate what's broken. Almost none of them have the slack to act on it [→ see DATA: *The 9% gap*, above]. *That's the trap.* The workers who could reform the system are the workers most consumed by running it.

If your team can't tell you what's vestigial — what would happen if a specific recurring activity were canceled — that's not because they don't know. It's because they don't have the airspace to think about it.

## Where the latent value lives

Here's the move that matters: **the value AI unlocks doesn't live where any one team is currently working. It lives in the gaps between teams.**

Three flavours, all worth scanning your business for.

**Between functions.** The work that doesn't fit any one team's mandate. Customer-success knows the product is hard for new users in a specific way. Product-engineering owns the fix. Marketing owns the messaging. The fix lives in the intersection of all three. In the old shop, it waits in queues — exactly because no one team's KPIs reward driving it.

> **CASE — FabricationCo's quoting workflow**
>
> A Fortune-1000 manufacturer (Bain client; OpenAI partnership) re-architected its bid-quoting process around AI.
>
> **Old workflow**: design engineers spent hours producing initial designs for *every* bid — including the >50% that wouldn't convert.
> **New workflow**: 20-minute cost estimates by *non-designers* for early-stage bids; full engineering reserved for higher-probability opportunities.
>
> **Result: ~15× faster quote generation. +10 percentage-point win rate within three months. ~$30M additional profit on track.**
>
> The intersection that got resolved? Sales, design, and operations had each been doing 100% of their work assuming the others would also do 100% of theirs. Most of the value was in **letting non-designers do the early-stage work** — which meant trusting AI on the parts a designer used to manually own.
>
> *Source: Dutt, Chatterji et al., "How to Move from AI Experimentation to AI Transformation," HBR.org, April 2026.*

**Between products and customer categories.** Value propositions that combine offerings you already have but never bundled because the bundling required cross-team negotiation no one had time for [→ see CASE: *Lowe's Mylow Companion*, below].

> **CASE — Lowe's Mylow Companion**
>
> Lowe's didn't invent a new product to launch Mylow Companion in 1,700+ U.S. stores. They bundled three things they already had: **stock retail product knowledge × associate floor presence × AI conversational ability.**
>
> **Results: online conversion rate more than doubled** when customers engage Mylow online. **+200 basis-point CSAT lift** when associates use the in-store companion.
>
> Nobody at Lowe's had the bandwidth to build the bundling before, because the three streams sat in different orgs. The intersection sat in plain sight, waiting to be picked up.
>
> *Source: Dutt, Chatterji et al., HBR.org, April 2026.*

**Between an existing process and the question of whether it should still exist.** The cheapest, fastest intersection of all. Pick a recurring activity you're spending serious attention on. Ask whether it exists because some specific historical resource was scarce. If the answer is yes, ask whether that scarcity still binds. **If the answer is no, you have just identified an intersection.**

## Six lessons from a team that already did it

Fiona Fung runs Claude Code engineering at Anthropic. In a recent talk on *running an AI-native engineering org* she describes the team-norms rewrite she led — and most of it generalises out of engineering into any business. Six points worth landing:

**1. Old processes don't die. Name the ones that can go.** Fung's third priority: *"people don't delete processes on their own; they pile new ones on top; name the ones that can go."* This is universal organizational housekeeping. Every team accumulates process around an old cost-balance. The work to surface what's vestigial is the work no one has time for; making that work *explicit and ritualized* is what changes it.

**2. The cost of asking a "dumb" question went to zero.** With AI ambient, a marketing manager understanding a manufacturing team's quoting workflow used to take hours of explanation; now it's a five-minute prompt. **The intersection becomes traversable.** People who used to be specialists in one domain can now show up at intersections without translation overhead.

**3. Build cheap, argue expensive.** When prototyping anything — a brief, a model, a process map — is fast, the *deliberation* about which approach to take is more expensive than just trying them. Fung's team generates three PRs instead of whiteboarding one. Translated to your business: stop deliberating in meetings about choices that AI could just produce three versions of.

**4. Roles blur. Intersections become real estate.** The cross-functional gap used to be where work *waited*. In the AI-native organization the gap is where work *gets done*. Whoever can stand at the gap and direct the synthesis is the new high-leverage role.

**5. The 6-month adoption test.** Fung's slide-10 challenge: *"if these don't shift in six months, adoption isn't working."* Pick three direction-of-travel signals appropriate to your function — cycle time on a key workflow, time-to-productivity for new staff, share of work that has AI in the loop. Commit to a six-month checkpoint. **If the signals haven't moved, you bought a license, not a technology.**

**6. Leaders stay close to the work.** Fung's rule: every manager on her team starts as an IC. Generalised: in fast-moving environments, the leader who hasn't done recent hands-on work is operating with stale models. The marketing director who hasn't briefed an AI agent themselves, the legal head who hasn't tried the contract-review prompt, the CFO who hasn't asked the model to forecast — all running on out-of-date intuition.

## What gets in the way

Three forces stop most mid-market businesses from picking up the intersection value.

**Resistance read as deviance, not data.** When a team resists the new workflow, the default is to push harder. The information-rich move is to ask which of four signals is firing [→ see FRAMEWORK: *Resistance as data*, below].

> **FRAMEWORK — Resistance as data**
>
> Ron Carucci (Navalent) names four resistance signal categories in HBR Digital, April 2026:
>
> - **Loss** — what people will give up that mattered to them
> - **Anxiety** — uncertainty about what comes next
> - **Lack of control** — being asked to change without input on the change
> - **Flaws in change** — *the team is telling you something is actually wrong with your reform*
>
> Three traps leaders fall into when they ignore the signal: **personalize** (treat resistance as individual deviance), **moralize** (cast it as a values failure), **rush to resolution** (push past it to the next agenda item).
>
> The fourth signal — *Flaws in change* — is where the intersection-unlocking information sits. The team is telling you the reform won't work in this domain. Most leaders suppress that signal under deadline pressure.
>
> *Source: Carucci, "Resistance as Data," HBR.org Digital, April 2026.*

**The micro-productivity trap.** Buying AI tools and getting individual task gains that don't translate to firm-level value — because the surrounding workflow still depends on tacit knowledge, manual handoffs, and legacy systems no one redesigned. The intersection-unlocking work *is* the workflow-redesign work. A firm that buys without redesigning never reaches the intersections; the AI just speeds up the running of the old shop.

**Theatre that calls itself work.** Fung's sharpest line: *"for most work it [the design doc] was theater."* The same is true in your business. Some non-trivial fraction of recurring activity is theatre — the appearance of value-producing work that consumes attention but produces no value the organization couldn't produce without it.

Fung tells one story worth keeping in your back pocket. A team she was on used to hold a weekly review — **50 people in a large room.** She noticed everyone was on their laptops the entire time, except for the moment when it was their turn to give a status report. Then they'd briefly look up, say the status, and go back down. She asked the simplest possible question: *"why are we having it?"* The room agreed it didn't earn its place. They canceled the meeting.

Most leaders don't audit their theatre because the theatre serves status, not just task. You can't ask a team to disclose its theatre wholesale — that puts everyone's status at risk at once. **What you can do is ask the simple question, one specific recurring activity at a time.** *"Why are we having this?"* — asked in plain English about a specific thing — rarely leaves the theatre standing. Asked in the abstract about everything, it leaves it untouched.

## A Monday-morning question

If you take one thing from this:

**Pick your most expensive recurring activity — the one that consumes the most attention from people you cannot replace. Ask whether it exists because some specific historical resource was scarce. If the answer is yes, ask whether that scarcity still binds. If the answer is no, you have just identified an intersection.**

The intersection is where two streams of work meet that the activity was preventing from meeting. Standing at the intersection is the work that's worth doing this quarter. **The freedom to stand there is what AI buys you — if you direct the freed capacity toward the intersection rather than toward more of the old shop running.**

Whether your business compounds or stalls in the late 2020s depends on which choice you make.
