# Preparedness, impacts, and responses of public health emergencies towards health security

## TL;DR

A scoping review synthesizing evidence on natural and human-made public health emergencies (armed conflicts, floods, disease outbreaks), their impacts on health systems, and the preparedness and response measures needed to strengthen health security across countries.

## Key findings

- Public health emergencies (PHEs) have variable intensity and types of impacts across countries due to contextual factors.
- Preparedness measures include early warning systems, stockpiling, training, and coordination mechanisms.
- Responses involve surge capacity activation, resource reallocation, and interagency collaboration.
- Health systems face interruption of service delivery and increased demand during PHEs.
- Evidence shows that pre-event planning and resilience building are critical determinants of response effectiveness.

## Implementation implications

- Health security strategies must balance preventive preparedness with adaptive response capacity.
- Countries need context-specific assessments of emergency types (natural disasters, conflict, epidemics) to tailor preparedness plans.
- Cross-sectoral coordination (health, defense, civil protection, logistics) is essential but often lacks institutional mechanisms.
- Capacity building in vulnerable countries requires long-term support, not just emergency response funding.
