# Natural hazards and climate change are not drivers of disasters

## TL;DR

This paper challenges the common framing that natural hazards and climate change are the primary drivers of disasters. Instead, it argues that vulnerability and exposure—shaped by human decision-making and development patterns—are the true drivers, with hazards and climate change acting as triggers.

## Key findings

- **Conceptual distinction**: Natural hazards (NHs) and climate change (CC) are triggers, not drivers of disasters.
- **True drivers**: Vulnerability (V) and Exposure (E) are the major drivers of disasters in most contexts.
- **Decision-making gap**: Current disaster response relies on scientific understanding of NHs and CC, but neglects V and E assessment.
- **Time-dependent risk**: Disaster risk changes over time due to cumulative effects of vulnerability, exposure, and extreme event intensity changes.
- **Risk reduction imperative**: Informed decision-making requires integrated knowledge across hazard science, vulnerability assessment, exposure mapping, and preventive action options.

## Implementation implications

- Disaster risk reduction policies must pivot focus from hazard monitoring to vulnerability mitigation and exposure management.
- Countries need improved systems for assessing and reducing vulnerability across populations and infrastructure.
- Public awareness campaigns should emphasize personal/community resilience rather than fear of extreme events alone.
- Climate adaptation must include explicit vulnerability reduction measures, not just hazard preparedness.
- Preventive disaster measures (land-use planning, building standards, social safety nets) yield higher returns than post-disaster response investment.
